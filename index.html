    By the time we ______ the station, the train had already departed.
    reach
    reached
    had reached
    have reached
    Explanation: The sentence uses past perfect ('had already departed') for the earlier action. The later action in the main clause takes simple past 'reached'. This follows the rule: past perfect for the earlier event, simple past for the later event.
    She ______ in this company for ten years before she got promoted.
    has worked
    was working
    had been working
    worked
    Explanation: Past perfect continuous 'had been working' is used to show a continuous action that started and continued up to another point in the past ('before she got promoted'). It emphasizes the duration of the activity before the past event.
    I wish I ______ harder for the examination last month.
    had studied
    have studied
    studied
    would study
    Explanation: 'I wish' followed by a past event requires past perfect tense to express regret about something that did not happen. 'Had studied' correctly conveys the unreal past wish.
    This time tomorrow, they ______ their final presentation to the board.
    will give
    are giving
    give
    will be giving
    Explanation: 'This time tomorrow' indicates an action that will be in progress at a specific point in the future. Future continuous tense 'will be giving' is the correct choice.
    Hardly had the match begun ______ it started to rain heavily.
    than
    when
    before
    then
    Explanation: The structure 'Hardly had...when' is a fixed correlative conjunction pair used with past perfect and simple past. 'No sooner...than' and 'Hardly/Scarcely...when' are standard patterns.
    By next December, we ______ our research project.
    will complete
    complete
    will have completed
    are completing
    Explanation: 'By next December' signals a deadline in the future by which an action will be finished. Future perfect tense 'will have completed' is used for actions completed before a specific future time.
    The manager told the employees that the company ______ new policies the previous week.
    had introduced
    has introduced
    introduced
    was introducing
    Explanation: In reported speech, when the reporting verb is in past tense ('told'), simple past becomes past perfect. Also 'the previous week' confirms the backshift. Hence 'had introduced' is correct.
    If she ______ the truth earlier, she would not have made that mistake.
    knows
    knew
    has known
    had known
    Explanation: This is a third conditional (past unreal condition). The if-clause requires past perfect 'had known' and the result clause uses 'would not have made'. It expresses regret about a past situation.
    Every morning, the sun ______ in the east. This is a universal truth.
    rose
    rises
    is rising
    has risen
    Explanation: Universal truths, scientific facts, and habitual actions are expressed in the simple present tense. 'Rises' is the correct simple present form.
    No sooner had the CEO finished his speech ______ the audience erupted in applause.
    when
    as
    than
    before
    Explanation: 'No sooner...than' is the correct correlative pair. The structure is: No sooner + had + subject + V3 + than + simple past. This is a fixed grammatical pattern tested frequently in competitive exams.
    She ______ three novels so far this year, and she plans to write two more.
    has written
    wrote
    had written
    is writing
    Explanation: 'So far this year' indicates an action that started in the past and continues to the present. Present perfect 'has written' is used with such time expressions to connect past actions to the present.
    While the students ______ the experiment, the lab equipment malfunctioned.
    conduct
    had conducted
    have conducted
    were conducting
    Explanation: 'While' introduces a continuous background action. Past continuous 'were conducting' is used for the longer ongoing action that was interrupted by the shorter action 'malfunctioned' in simple past.
    By the time the ambulance arrived, the patient ______.
    died
    had died
    has died
    was dying
    Explanation: 'By the time...arrived' (simple past) requires the earlier completed action to be in past perfect. The patient's death occurred before the ambulance's arrival, so 'had died' is correct.
    The professor said that the Earth ______ around the Sun.
    moved
    had moved
    moves
    was moving
    Explanation: Universal truths and scientific facts do not change tense in reported speech, even when the reporting verb is in the past. 'Moves' (simple present) is retained because it is a permanent fact.
    I ______ for you since 8 o'clock. Where have you been?
    have been waiting
    am waiting
    waited
    was waiting
    Explanation: 'Since 8 o'clock' with a present context requires present perfect continuous 'have been waiting'. This tense emphasizes the duration of an action that started in the past and continues to the present moment.
    If it ______ tomorrow, the outdoor ceremony will be postponed.
    will rain
    rained
    would rain
    rains
    Explanation: In first conditional sentences (real future possibility), the if-clause takes simple present tense, not future tense. 'If it rains' (simple present) + 'will be postponed' (simple future) is the correct structure.
    By 2030, scientists ______ on this project for over a decade.
    will work
    will have been working
    are working
    have worked
    Explanation: 'By 2030' + 'for over a decade' requires future perfect continuous tense. 'Will have been working' shows a continuous action that will still be ongoing up to that future point, emphasizing duration.
    It is high time the government ______ stricter environmental laws.
    implemented
    implements
    has implemented
    will implement
    Explanation: The expression 'It is high time' is followed by simple past tense (subjunctive use) to express that something should have been done already. 'Implemented' is the correct form despite referring to the present/future.
    She speaks French fluently as if she ______ a native speaker.
    is
    has been
    were
    will be
    Explanation: 'As if' / 'as though' expressing an unreal or hypothetical present situation requires the subjunctive mood, which uses 'were' for all subjects. This is a common competitive exam question on the subjunctive with tenses.
    The train ______ at 6:30 AM every day according to the new timetable.
    is departing
    departs
    will depart
    departed
    Explanation: Scheduled or timetabled events use simple present tense even when referring to the future. 'Departs' is correct because it describes a fixed schedule. This rule applies to trains, flights, classes, and other timetabled events.

    Advanced Tenses Quiz - Set 2

    ENGLISH HARD 20 Questions

    Elevate your grammar preparation with this second set of competitive-level English tenses questions. This quiz features intricate sentences testing past continuous vs past perfect, present perfect vs simple past, future perfect continuous, and mixed conditional structures. Designed for aspirants of SSC CGL, IBPS PO, SBI Clerk, UPSC, GRE, GMAT, IELTS, and TOEFL, every question mirrors actual exam patterns. You will encounter reported speech tense shifts, time clause rules, stative verb exceptions, and the subjunctive mood. Understanding when to use each tense form is critical for scoring high in the grammar section of any competitive exam. This quiz helps you identify common traps and errors that examiners use to test deep grammatical knowledge. Each question comes with a detailed explanation that clarifies the rule behind the correct answer. Practice regularly with these quizzes to develop an intuitive feel for correct tense usage. Whether you are a student, job aspirant, or English language enthusiast, this quiz will push your limits and prepare you for the toughest grammar challenges.

    He ______ the book by the time his friend visits him next week.
    reads
    read
    will have read
    has read
    Explanation: 'By the time...visits' (simple present in the time clause for future reference) requires future perfect 'will have read' in the main clause to show an action completed before a future event.
    Scarcely had the witness begun to speak ______ the defense lawyer objected.
    when
    than
    before
    as
    Explanation: 'Scarcely had...when' is the correct correlative structure, similar to 'Hardly had...when'. The earlier action uses past perfect (inverted) and the later action uses simple past.
    The children ______ in the garden when their mother called them for dinner.
    played
    have played
    had played
    were playing
    Explanation: 'When their mother called' (simple past) interrupts an ongoing background action. Past continuous 'were playing' is used for the action in progress at the time of the interruption.
    She ______ to New York three times since she moved to America.
    went
    has been
    had been
    was going
    Explanation: 'Since she moved' connects a past event to the present. Present perfect 'has been' is required with 'since' when the reference point extends to the present. 'Three times' quantifies the repeated action.
    I would rather you ______ the report yesterday instead of today.
    submit
    submitted
    had submitted
    have submitted
    Explanation: 'Would rather' + different subject + past perfect is used to express a preference about a past action that did not happen. 'Had submitted' is the correct form for past unreal preference with 'yesterday'.
    They ______ each other for years before they finally decided to start a business together.
    had known
    knew
    have known
    are knowing
    Explanation: 'Before they decided' (simple past) establishes a past reference. The action of knowing was completed before that past point, requiring past perfect 'had known'. 'Know' is a stative verb, so continuous form is avoided.
    Look at those dark clouds! It ______ any moment now.
    rains
    rained
    has rained
    is going to rain
    Explanation: When there is present evidence (dark clouds) suggesting a near-future event, 'be going to' is used for predictions based on evidence. 'Is going to rain' is more appropriate than 'will rain' in this context.
    The teacher asked the students whether they ______ their homework.
    complete
    had completed
    have completed
    will complete
    Explanation: In reported speech with a past reporting verb ('asked'), present perfect 'have completed' shifts to past perfect 'had completed'. The indirect question also uses statement word order.
    We ______ here for twenty minutes when the bus finally arrived.
    had been standing
    have been standing
    were standing
    stood
    Explanation: 'For twenty minutes' indicates duration, and 'when the bus finally arrived' is a past reference point. Past perfect continuous 'had been standing' is used for a continuous action leading up to another past event.
    If I ______ you, I would accept the job offer without hesitation.
    am
    was
    were
    had been
    Explanation: This is a second conditional (present unreal condition). The if-clause uses the subjunctive 'were' for all subjects, including 'I'. 'If I were you' is the standard form in formal English and competitive exams.
    She ______ in this apartment since 2015 and has no plans to move.
    lived
    has been living
    is living
    had lived
    Explanation: 'Since 2015' with 'has no plans to move' (present context) requires present perfect continuous 'has been living' to emphasize the ongoing nature and duration of the action from the past to now.
    The concert ______ at 7 PM sharp. Don't be late!
    will start
    is going to start
    started
    starts
    Explanation: Scheduled events with fixed timetables use simple present tense for future reference. 'Starts' indicates a fixed, official schedule for the concert.
    He behaves as though he ______ the owner of this company.
    were
    is
    has been
    will be
    Explanation: 'As though' (like 'as if') expressing an unreal present situation requires the subjunctive 'were'. He is not actually the owner, so the hypothetical/unreal form is needed.
    By the end of this month, I ______ at this company for exactly five years.
    work
    am working
    will have been working
    have worked
    Explanation: 'By the end of this month' + 'for exactly five years' requires future perfect continuous 'will have been working'. This tense emphasizes the duration of an ongoing action up to a specific future point.
    It ______ continuously for three days, and the rivers are flooding.
    rained
    has been raining
    was raining
    had been raining
    Explanation: 'For three days' with a present result ('rivers are flooding') requires present perfect continuous 'has been raining'. The action started in the past and its effects continue to the present.
    Had I known about the delay, I ______ at home longer.
    would have stayed
    will stay
    stayed
    would stay
    Explanation: This is an inverted third conditional. 'Had I known' = 'If I had known'. The result clause requires 'would have + past participle'. 'Would have stayed' expresses the unrealized past consequence.
    This is the first time I ______ such a magnificent waterfall.
    saw
    see
    am seeing
    have seen
    Explanation: The structure 'This is the first/second time + present perfect' is a fixed pattern. 'Have seen' is correct because the experience connects to the present moment. If the sentence used 'was', past perfect 'had seen' would be needed.
    When I entered the room, everybody ______ at something on the screen.
    stared
    has stared
    was staring
    stares
    Explanation: 'When I entered' (simple past) describes the moment of arrival. The ongoing action at that moment uses past continuous 'was staring'. 'Everybody' takes a singular verb form.
    The deadline is next Friday. We ______ the project by then, no matter what.
    finish
    will have finished
    finished
    are finishing
    Explanation: 'By then' (by next Friday) indicates a future deadline. Future perfect 'will have finished' expresses the completion of an action before a specified future time.
    He ______ about the news before I told him; someone had already informed him.
    had heard
    heard
    has heard
    hears
    Explanation: Two past events require past perfect for the earlier one and simple past for the later one. He heard the news (earlier — past perfect 'had heard') before I told him (later — simple past).

    Advanced Tenses Quiz - Set 3

    ENGLISH HARD 20 Questions

    This third set of competitive-level tenses questions dives deeper into complex grammatical structures that frequently appear in government job exams, banking exams, and international English proficiency tests. You will face questions on sequence of tenses in complex sentences, tense consistency in paragraphs, tricky time adverb placements, and subtle distinctions between present perfect and simple past. The quiz also covers advanced topics like the use of tenses in conditional clauses, wish clauses, and time clauses with conjunctions such as 'before', 'after', 'until', 'as soon as', and 'by the time'. Each question is crafted to simulate the difficulty level of SSC, IBPS, RRB, UPSC Prelims, CAT, and other national-level competitive examinations. Detailed explanations accompany each answer to help you understand the underlying grammar rule. Regular practice with these quizzes will significantly improve your ability to identify correct tense usage in fill-in-the-blank, error spotting, and sentence correction questions.     As soon as the bell ______, the students rushed out of the classroom.
    rings
    had rung
    rang
    was ringing
    Explanation: 'As soon as' introduces a time clause. When both actions happened in the past in quick succession, simple past is used in the time clause. 'Rang' (simple past) + 'rushed' (simple past) describes two sequential past actions.
    She ______ for the interview all week, so she feels confident now.
    prepared
    has been preparing
    was preparing
    had prepared
    Explanation: 'All week' + 'feels confident now' connects a recent continuous activity to a present result. Present perfect continuous 'has been preparing' correctly links the past ongoing effort to the current feeling.
    I ______ never ______ such a thrilling movie before in my life.
    have...seen
    had...seen
    did...see
    was...seeing
    Explanation: 'Never...before in my life' with a present perspective requires present perfect. 'Have never seen' expresses a life experience up to the current moment. This is a classic present perfect usage with 'never' and 'before'.
    The workers ______ the bridge for two years before the government halted the project.
    built
    were building
    have been building
    had been building
    Explanation: 'For two years' (duration) + 'before the government halted' (past reference point) requires past perfect continuous. 'Had been building' shows the ongoing construction that continued up to the moment the project was halted.
    Unless you ______ hard, you will not pass the competitive exam.
    will study
    study
    studied
    have studied
    Explanation: 'Unless' (meaning 'if...not') introduces a conditional clause. Like 'if', the unless-clause takes simple present tense for future conditions. 'Unless you study' + 'you will not pass' is the correct first conditional pattern.
    My grandfather ______ in this village all his life. He was born here and died here.
    has lived
    is living
    lived
    had lived
    Explanation: 'He was born here and died here' tells us the grandfather is no longer alive. When referring to a completed life, simple past 'lived' is used. Present perfect cannot be used for deceased persons whose connection to the present is broken.
    It is essential that every student ______ the form before the deadline.
    submit
    submits
    submitted
    will submit
    Explanation: After expressions of necessity like 'It is essential that', the subjunctive mood is used. The subjunctive uses the base form of the verb 'submit' (without -s) regardless of the subject. This is a mandative subjunctive.
    She told me that she ______ to Paris the previous summer.
    goes
    has gone
    went
    had gone
    Explanation: In reported speech with past reporting verb 'told', simple past 'went' becomes past perfect 'had gone'. 'The previous summer' (backshifted from 'last summer') confirms the past perfect is required.
    The plane ______ off at 9 AM tomorrow. We should reach the airport by 7 AM.
    will take
    takes
    is taking
    took
    Explanation: Scheduled departures of transport (flights, trains, buses) use simple present tense for future reference. 'Takes off at 9 AM tomorrow' follows the timetable rule where present simple expresses future scheduled events.
    Were she to ______ for the position, she would undoubtedly be selected.
    applying
    applied
    apply
    applies
    Explanation: 'Were she to apply' is a formal inverted conditional structure equivalent to 'If she were to apply'. After 'were + subject + to', the base form of the verb is used. This is a second conditional in formal/literary style.
    I ______ him since we graduated from college in 2010.
    have not seen
    did not see
    had not seen
    do not see
    Explanation: 'Since we graduated in 2010' connects a past event to the present. Present perfect 'have not seen' is required with 'since' to show the action extends from the past to now. The speaker has still not seen the person.
    After the guests ______, we cleaned up the entire house.
    leave
    are leaving
    left
    had left
    Explanation: 'After' introduces the earlier of two past events. The earlier event takes past perfect 'had left', and the later event takes simple past 'cleaned'. This maintains the correct sequence of tenses for past events.
    They ______ married for twenty-five years next April.
    are
    will have been
    have been
    were
    Explanation: 'Next April' is a future reference point, and 'for twenty-five years' shows duration up to that point. Future perfect 'will have been' indicates a state that will reach a milestone at a future time.
    I would rather she ______ the truth right now.
    told
    tells
    tell
    has told
    Explanation: 'Would rather' + different subject + past tense is used for present or future preferences. 'Told' (simple past form, subjunctive use) is correct even though the time reference is 'right now'. This is a common competitive exam pattern.
    The moment the results ______ announced, the crowd went wild.
    are
    have been
    were
    had been
    Explanation: 'The moment' introduces a time clause describing simultaneous past actions. 'Were announced' (simple past passive) + 'went wild' (simple past) correctly shows two actions happening at the same past moment.
    If only I ______ more careful with my investments last year!
    am
    was
    were
    had been
    Explanation: 'If only' expressing regret about a past action requires past perfect. 'Had been' is correct because 'last year' clearly indicates a past time. 'If only + past perfect' expresses unfulfilled past wishes.
    Water ______ at 100 degrees Celsius under normal atmospheric pressure.
    boiled
    boils
    is boiling
    has boiled
    Explanation: Scientific facts and universal truths always use simple present tense. 'Boils' is the correct form because this is an established physical fact that is always true, regardless of the context.
    She ______ her keys. She is looking everywhere for them.
    has lost
    lost
    had lost
    loses
    Explanation: The present result ('She is looking everywhere') connects to a past action. Present perfect 'has lost' links the past event of losing to the present consequence of searching. The keys are still missing.
    He ______ in the library at 4 PM yesterday when I saw him.
    studied
    has studied
    was studying
    had studied
    Explanation: A specific time in the past ('at 4 PM yesterday') + 'when I saw him' indicates an action in progress at that exact moment. Past continuous 'was studying' is used for ongoing actions at a particular past time.
    By this time next year, she ______ her doctorate.
    completes
    will have completed
    completed
    has completed
    Explanation: 'By this time next year' sets a future deadline. Future perfect 'will have completed' shows an action that will be finished before that specific future point. This is the classic 'by + future time' pattern.

    I ______ to the gym every morning when I was living in Mumbai.
    go
    am going
    have gone
    used to go
    Explanation: 'Used to go' describes a habitual past action that no longer occurs. 'When I was living in Mumbai' confirms the action belongs entirely to the past. 'Used to' is specifically for past habits that have been discontinued.
    Seldom ______ such an eloquent speech in the parliament.
    has one heard
    one has heard
    one hears
    did one heard
    Explanation: Negative adverbs like 'seldom', 'rarely', 'never' at the beginning of a sentence trigger subject-auxiliary inversion. 'Has one heard' is the correct inverted form with present perfect, indicating an experience up to now.
    She will wait until the rain ______.
    will stop
    would stop
    stops
    stopped
    Explanation: In time clauses introduced by 'until', 'when', 'before', 'after', 'as soon as', simple present is used for future reference, not 'will'. 'Until the rain stops' is the correct pattern with the main clause in future tense.
    At this time last Monday, we ______ for our final examinations.
    studied
    were studying
    had studied
    have been studying
    Explanation: 'At this time last Monday' specifies an exact moment in the past. Past continuous 'were studying' is used for actions in progress at a specific past time, similar to how present continuous works for the current moment.
    The moment I finish this report, I ______ you a copy.
    send
    sent
    am sending
    will send
    Explanation: 'The moment I finish' is a time clause using simple present for future. The main clause requires simple future 'will send'. The time clause rule: simple present in the time clause, future tense in the main clause.
    He ______ three cups of coffee before the meeting even started.
    had drunk
    drank
    has drunk
    was drinking
    Explanation: 'Before the meeting even started' (simple past) establishes a past reference. The action of drinking three cups occurred before that, requiring past perfect 'had drunk' for the earlier completed action.
    She asked me where I ______ the previous night.
    go
    went
    had gone
    have gone
    Explanation: In reported speech with past reporting verb 'asked', the simple past 'went' shifts to past perfect 'had gone'. 'The previous night' (backshifted from 'last night') further confirms the need for past perfect.
    Not only ______ the exam, but she also topped the state.
    she passed
    did she pass
    she has passed
    has she passed
    Explanation: 'Not only' at the beginning of a clause triggers inversion. 'Did she pass' is the correct inverted form with simple past. The structure is: Not only + auxiliary + subject + verb. The second clause follows normal order with 'but also'.
    We ______ forward to meeting you at the conference next week.
    are looking
    looked
    look
    have looked
    Explanation: 'Look forward to' expressing anticipation for a planned future event uses present continuous. 'Are looking forward to' indicates a present feeling about a future arrangement ('next week').
    If she had taken the medicine on time, she ______ better by now.
    will feel
    felt
    has felt
    would be feeling
    Explanation: This is a mixed conditional: past condition (If + past perfect 'had taken') with a present result ('by now'). The result clause uses 'would be + present participle' to show an ongoing present consequence of an unreal past condition.
    He ______ as a teacher for thirty years and is planning to retire next month.
    worked
    is working
    has been working
    had worked
    Explanation: 'For thirty years' (duration from past to present) + 'is planning to retire' (present context) requires present perfect continuous 'has been working'. The action started in the past and continues to the present with ongoing relevance.
    It was the worst disaster that ______ ever struck the region.
    has
    had
    have
    was
    Explanation: 'It was' (past tense main clause) with superlative structure requires past perfect in the relative clause. 'Had ever struck' shows the superlative experience up to that past point. With 'It is', present perfect would be used.
    I ______ to inform you that your application has been rejected.
    regret
    am regretting
    regretted
    have regretted
    Explanation: 'Regret' used in the sense of formally expressing sorrow is a performative verb used in simple present. 'I regret to inform you' is a fixed formal expression. 'Regret' as a stative verb does not typically take continuous form.
    The committee ______ their decision before the next session begins.
    announces
    announced
    is announcing
    will have announced
    Explanation: 'Before the next session begins' (present simple for future in time clause) requires future perfect in the main clause. 'Will have announced' shows completion before the future event. This follows the 'before + present simple / main clause + future perfect' pattern.
    I ______ about changing my career lately, but I haven't made a decision yet.
    think
    thought

    The suspect denied that he ______ present at the scene of the crime.
    is
    was
    had been
    has been
    Explanation: 'Denied' (past reporting verb) + the event in question occurred before the denial. Past perfect 'had been' is needed because the presence at the crime scene was before the act of denying. Double backshift applies here.
    Where ______ you ______ when the earthquake struck yesterday?
    were...standing
    did...stand
    have...stood
    had...stood
    Explanation: 'When the earthquake struck yesterday' (simple past) interrupts an action in progress. Past continuous 'were you standing' asks about the ongoing action at the moment of the earthquake.
    The novel I ______ currently ______ is based on a true story from World War II.
    was...reading
    had...read
    have...read
    am...reading
    Explanation: 'Currently' indicates the present moment. Present continuous 'am reading' is used for actions happening right now or around the current time. The novel is being read at this present period.
    The moment he ______ the truth, he will understand our decision.
    will learn
    learns
    learned
    has learned
    Explanation: 'The moment' introduces a time clause for future reference. Like 'when', 'as soon as', etc., it takes simple present 'learns' (not future tense) while the main clause takes future 'will understand'.
    If she ______ harder, she would have won the gold medal.
    trains
    trained
    had trained
    was training
    Explanation: Third conditional: 'If + past perfect' (had trained) + 'would have + past participle' (would have won). This expresses regret about a past action: the training was insufficient, and the gold medal was not won.
    He ______ the book on the table and then left the room without saying a word.
    laid
    lied
    lain
    lay
    Explanation: 'Lay-laid-laid' (transitive: to put something down) vs 'Lie-lay-lain' (intransitive: to recline). The subject placed the book (transitive action), so past tense of 'lay' is 'laid'. This is one of the most commonly tested verb form/tense questions.

    Advanced Tenses Quiz - Set 9

    ENGLISH HARD 20 Questions

    Set 9 continues our comprehensive exploration of English tenses at the competitive examination level. This quiz targets advanced constructions that often stump even well-prepared candidates: tense usage with perception verbs, causative constructions, and tense in adjectival clauses. You will also face questions on the difference between 'was going to' and 'would' for unfulfilled plans, tense agreement in parallel structures, and the use of present perfect with superlatives and ordinals. These question types frequently appear in exams like SSC MTS, RRB NTPC, CTET, DSSSB, KVS, and state teacher eligibility tests. The quiz also covers tense-related errors commonly found in sentence correction and error detection sections. By mastering these concepts, you will be able to quickly identify incorrect tense usage and select the correct form under time pressure. The detailed explanations serve as concise grammar reference notes that you can revisit before your exam. Combine this quiz with our other sets for a complete tenses preparation package that covers every possible exam question pattern.

    She is the most talented singer I ______ ever ______.
    had...heard
    did...hear
    have...heard
    was...hearing
    Explanation: Superlative + present perfect is the standard pattern. 'She is' (present) + 'the most talented...I have ever heard' connects the life experience to the present. Present perfect 'have heard' is used with superlatives in present contexts.
    The government ______ several measures to control inflation last quarter.
    takes
    took
    has taken
    is taking
    Explanation: 'Last quarter' is a definite past time reference. Simple past 'took' is correct for completed actions at specified past times. Present perfect cannot be used with 'last quarter' as it marks a finished time period.
    They ______ for the hiking trip when it suddenly started snowing.
    were preparing
    prepared
    have prepared
    had been prepared
    Explanation: 'When it suddenly started snowing' (simple past) interrupted an ongoing action. Past continuous 'were preparing' describes the action in progress at the time of the interruption.
    I was going to call you, but I ______ my phone at home.
    leave
    have left
    am leaving
    had left
    Explanation: 'Was going to call' expresses an unfulfilled past intention. The reason (leaving the phone) happened before the intention, requiring past perfect 'had left'. The phone was forgotten (earlier) before the planned call (later).
    Until you ______ me a written apology, I refuse to continue this conversation.
    will give
    gave
    give
    are giving
    Explanation: 'Until' introduces a time clause for future reference. Simple present 'give' is used in the time clause (not future tense). The main clause 'I refuse' (simple present) expresses a firm decision.
    The patient ______ steadily since the new treatment was started last month.
    improved
    has been improving
    was improving
    had improved
    Explanation: 'Since...last month' connects the past to the present. Present perfect continuous 'has been improving' emphasizes the ongoing gradual process of improvement that started in the past and continues now. 'Steadily' reinforces the continuous nature.
    He insisted that he ______ innocent and demanded a fair trial.
    was
    is
    has been
    be
    Explanation: 'Insisted' here means 'claimed/maintained' (not demanded), so it follows regular reported speech rules rather than the subjunctive. With past reporting verb 'insisted', 'is' shifts to 'was'. Compare: 'He insisted that he be released' (subjunctive — demand sense).
    My mother ______ dinner by the time I get home from work today.
    cooks
    cooked
    is cooking
    will have cooked
    Explanation: 'By the time I get home' (simple present for future in time clause) + future completion requires future perfect. 'Will have cooked' means the cooking will be finished before the arrival home.
    The athletes ______ rigorously for months before the Olympics begin.
    trained
    are training
    will have been training
    had trained
    Explanation: 'For months' (duration) + 'before the Olympics begin' (future reference using simple present) requires future perfect continuous. 'Will have been training' shows an ongoing action leading up to a future event, emphasizing the duration of preparation.
    She acted as if nothing ______ between them the previous night.
    happens
    had happened
    has happened
    is happening
    Explanation: 'As if' + past unreal reference ('the previous night') requires past perfect. 'Had happened' is used because the event (or non-event she pretends about) occurred before the past moment of acting. This is past subjunctive usage.
    Every student in the class ______ required to submit the thesis by December.
    is
    are
    were
    have been
    Explanation: 'Every student' takes a singular verb. 'Is required' (simple present passive) is correct. 'Every' emphasizes each individual student, making the subject grammatically singular regardless of the number of students.
    We were shocked to learn that the company ______ bankrupt the week before.
    goes
    went
    has gone
    had gone
    Explanation: 'Were shocked' (past) + 'the week before' (time before that past moment) requires past perfect 'had gone'. The bankruptcy occurred before the shock, necessitating past perfect for the earlier event.
    How long ______ you ______ English before you moved to Australia?
    did...study
    have...studied
    had...been studying
    were...studying
    Explanation: 'How long' asks about duration. 'Before you moved' (past reference point) requires past perfect continuous 'had been studying' to express the continuous action leading up to the past event of moving.
    The chairman proposed that the meeting ______ adjourned until the following week.
    is
    be
    was
    will be
    Explanation: 'Proposed that' triggers the mandative subjunctive. The base form 'be' is used (not 'is', 'was', or 'will be'). 'The meeting be adjourned' follows the formal subjunctive pattern required after verbs of proposal, suggestion, and recommendation.
    Next Monday at this time, the students ______ their final semester examination.
    will be writing
    write
    wrote
    have written
    Explanation: 'Next Monday at this time' indicates a specific moment in the future when an action will be in progress. Future continuous 'will be writing' is used for actions that will be ongoing at a particular future time.
    None of the students ______ submitted the project report yet.
    have
    are
    has
    had
    Explanation: 'None of the students' can take singular or plural verb, but in formal/competitive English, 'none' is often treated as singular. 'Has submitted' with 'yet' uses present perfect tense. 'Yet' is a present perfect marker indicating 'up to now'.
    She promised that she ______ return the book the next day.
    will
    would
    shall
    can
    Explanation: In reported speech with past reporting verb 'promised', 'will' becomes 'would'. 'The next day' (backshifted from 'tomorrow') confirms reported speech. 'Would return' is the correct reported form of 'will return'.
    I ______ the movie before, so I didn't want to watch it again.
    see
    saw
    have seen
    had seen
    Explanation: 'Didn't want' (simple past) is the later event. Seeing the movie happened before not wanting to watch it again. Past perfect 'had seen' is required for the earlier of two past events.
    The population of the world ______ rapidly since the Industrial Revolution.
    has been growing
    grew
    was growing
    had been growing
    Explanation: 'Since the Industrial Revolution' connects a historical past event to the present trend. Present perfect continuous 'has been growing' emphasizes the ongoing, continuous nature of population growth from the past to now.
    He spoke as though he ______ an expert on the subject, but he knew nothing.
    is
    has been
    were
    will be
    Explanation: 'As though' + unreal present situation uses the subjunctive 'were' (for all subjects). 'He knew nothing' confirms the situation is contrary to fact. 'Were an expert' is the formal subjunctive expressing an unreal comparison at that past time.

    Advanced Tenses Quiz - Set 10

    ENGLISH HARD 20 Questions

    Set 10 marks the halfway point of our extensive tenses quiz collection and brings a refined selection of competition-level questions that test the most frequently examined tense patterns. This set features challenging questions on tense usage with stative verbs that occasionally take continuous form (like 'think', 'have', 'see', 'taste'), the historical present in narratives, and tense in newspaper headlines vs full sentences. Questions also cover the distinction between 'already', 'just', 'yet', 'still' with perfect tenses, and the use of tenses in formal letters and emails. This quiz is essential preparation for SBI PO, RBI Assistant, LIC AAO, EPFO, and other financial sector exams where English grammar carries significant weightage. The questions simulate the error spotting and fill-in-the-blank formats that dominate these exams. Each explanation breaks down the grammar rule step by step, making complex concepts easy to understand and remember. Test yourself under timed conditions to build exam-day readiness and identify weak areas for targeted revision.

    I ______ what you mean. There's no need to explain further.
    am seeing
    see
    have seen
    was seeing
    Explanation: 'See' meaning 'understand' is a stative verb and uses simple present tense, not continuous. 'I see' = 'I understand'. Stative verbs like 'see' (understand), 'know', 'believe', 'belong' don't normally take continuous form.
    This is the first time I ______ a live concert by this band.
    attend
    attended
    was attending
    have attended
    Explanation: 'This is the first time + present perfect' is a fixed pattern. 'Have attended' connects the new experience to the present. If the sentence were 'That was the first time', past perfect 'had attended' would be needed.
    The soup ______ delicious. What ingredients did you use?
    tastes
    is tasting
    tasted
    has tasted
    Explanation: 'Taste' used as a linking verb (meaning 'has the flavor of') is stative and takes simple present 'tastes'. Compare: 'She is tasting the soup' (active, voluntary action — continuous allowed). The question context confirms a stative use.
    He has just ______ from a long illness and needs rest.
    recover
    recovering
    recovered
    recovers
    Explanation: 'Has just recovered' uses present perfect with 'just' to indicate a very recent completed action. The past participle 'recovered' is required after 'has'. 'Just' emphasizes the recency of the recovery.
    She ______ a bath when the doorbell rang, so she couldn't answer immediately.
    has
    was having
    had
    has had
    Explanation: 'Have a bath' is an action verb (not stative), so continuous form is acceptable. 'Was having' (past continuous) describes the bath in progress when the doorbell rang (simple past interruption).
    She still ______ not ______ the money I lent her six months ago.
    did...return
    does...return
    is...returning
    has...returned
    Explanation: 'Still' with 'not' in present perfect indicates an action expected but not yet completed. 'Has still not returned' shows the money remains unreturned from the past lending to the present. 'Six months ago' is the starting point.
    In 1969, Neil Armstrong ______ the first human to walk on the Moon.
    became
    has become
    becomes
    had become
    Explanation: 'In 1969' is a specific past date. Simple past 'became' is used for completed historical events at definite past times. Present perfect cannot be used with specific past dates.
    They ______ already ______ the contract before the lawyer arrived.
    have...signed
    are...signing
    had...signed
    were...signing
    Explanation: 'Before the lawyer arrived' (simple past) is the later event. Signing the contract occurred before that, requiring past perfect 'had already signed'. 'Already' emphasizes the completion before the expected time.
    I ______ of buying a new car for months, but I can't afford one yet.
    think
    have been thinking
    thought
    had thought
    Explanation: 'For months' + 'can't afford one yet' (present context) requires present perfect continuous. 'Have been thinking' shows the ongoing consideration. 'Think' meaning 'consider' (not 'believe') can take continuous form.
    We ______ in this house for ten years now. We love the neighborhood.
    have lived
    lived
    are living
    had lived
    Explanation: 'For ten years now' connects past to present. Present perfect 'have lived' is correct. 'Live' as a stative verb often takes simple present perfect rather than continuous, though both are acceptable. The present context confirms ongoing residence.
    If I ______ rich, I would donate half my wealth to charity.
    am
    will be
    have been
    were
    Explanation: Second conditional (present unreal condition): 'If + were' (subjunctive) + 'would + base verb'. 'Were' is used for all subjects in the subjunctive after 'if' in formal English. The speaker is not actually rich.
    She ______ her homework. Now she can go out and play.
    finishes
    finished
    has finished
    was finishing
    Explanation: 'Now she can go out' is a present result of a recently completed action. Present perfect 'has finished' links the completed homework to the present freedom. The focus is on the present consequence of the past action.
    The professor emphasized that honesty ______ the best policy.
    was
    is
    had been
    would be
    Explanation: Proverbs and universal truths do not change tense in reported speech. Despite the past reporting verb 'emphasized', 'is' (simple present) is retained because 'honesty is the best policy' is a timeless truth/proverb.
    She ______ her grandmother every Sunday when she was a child.
    would visit
    visits
    has visited
    is visiting
    Explanation: 'Would + base verb' describes repeated past habits/actions. 'Would visit' = 'used to visit'. 'Every Sunday' + 'when she was a child' confirms a repeated past action. 'Would' for past habit is more literary than 'used to'.
    She ______ about her promotion all day. She can't concentrate on anything else.
    thinks
    thought
    had thought
    has been thinking
    Explanation: 'All day' + 'can't concentrate' (present result) requires present perfect continuous. 'Has been thinking' shows the continuous mental activity from the past to now with a visible present effect.
    By the time he retires, he ______ in this organization for forty years.
    works
    worked
    will have worked
    has worked
    Explanation: 'By the time he retires' (future reference) + 'for forty years' (duration) requires future perfect 'will have worked'. This expresses the total duration of work that will be accumulated by the retirement date.
    Ravi said that he ______ not been feeling well for several days.
    has
    had
    is
    was
    Explanation: In reported speech with past reporting verb 'said', present perfect continuous 'has not been feeling' shifts to past perfect continuous 'had not been feeling'. 'Had' is the correct auxiliary for the past perfect continuous backshift.
    I ______ this book belongs to you. Is that correct?
    believe
    am believing
    believed
    have believed
    Explanation: 'Believe' is a stative verb expressing a mental state. It takes simple present tense 'believe', not continuous form. 'Am believing' is grammatically incorrect in standard English because stative verbs don't take -ing form.
    She ______ for this moment her entire life, and she finally achieved her dream.
    waits
    is waiting
    was waiting
    had been waiting
    Explanation: 'Finally achieved' (simple past) is the reference point. 'Her entire life' (duration) + before achieving (past) requires past perfect continuous 'had been waiting'. This shows the long continuous wait that ended when the dream was achieved.
    They ______ to the new office by next Monday if everything goes as planned.
    shift
    shifted
    will have shifted
    are shifting
    Explanation: 'By next Monday' is a future deadline. 'If everything goes as planned' uses simple present for future condition. Future perfect 'will have shifted' expresses the action that will be completed before the deadline.
